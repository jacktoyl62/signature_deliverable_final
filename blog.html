Why Community Still Matters

Posted March 15, 2026 • By The Village Team

Community

I stopped by Juniper & Ash last Thursday night, mostly because I'd heard it just opened and wanted to see what the space felt like. It sits on the corner of Maple and 3rd, and by the time I got there, most of the tables were already full. I grabbed a seat near the front where Marcus Reed was already set up with a coffee. He mentioned he had recently moved from Charlotte and had been trying to get a better sense of what was happening locally.

The conversation started the way these usually do—quick introductions, what everyone's been working on—but it didn't stay surface-level for long. Marcus brought up an idea he'd been sitting on: a recurring event series that felt more consistent than what's currently out there. Turning Opportunities Into Action

Posted March 10, 2026 • By Community Member

Opportunities

I made it over to the Spring Night Market on Saturday night, right around 6:30, just as things were starting to fill in. It stretched a few blocks near Lakeview—food trucks on one side, vendor tables on the other, and a steady line of people moving through the middle. By the time the sun went down, it was packed. I spent a while walking through the booths and eventually stopped at a candle setup run by Elena Cruz.

She told me it was her first time at a larger market like this and that she wasn't sure what to expect. By the time I got there, she had already sold through more than half of what she brought. While I was there, Malik Johnson stopped by her table. He manages operations for a local retail space and had noticed the traffic she was getting. They started talking about how she makes everything herself, how long it takes, and what it would look like to produce at a larger scale...

Building Sustainable Community Strategies

Posted March 5, 2026 • By Sarah Chen

Strategy

The key to building something that lasts isn't found in big announcements or perfect planning from day one. It's in the small decisions made along the way, by people who show up consistently and listen to what's actually needed. When we look at the most successful community initiatives, we find they share common patterns: they started small, they stayed flexible, and they built trust before asking for commitment.

Over the past several months, we've been documenting what works and what doesn't in community building. We've watched projects launch with great fanfare only to fade away, and we've seen quiet efforts grow into something significant. The difference often comes down to strategy—not strategy in the corporate sense of quarterly goals and metrics, but in the daily choices about priorities and relationships.
